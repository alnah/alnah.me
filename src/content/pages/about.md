# About

I help people automate, simplify, and structure the way they build, work, and learn.

## Common issues

A lot of friction in work, study, or personal projects does not come from a lack of effort. It comes from not knowing what to learn first, how different skills fit together, or how to get useful results before the investment feels too high.

I know that experience well. When I started learning programming to make my life as a French teacher easier, the cost felt bigger than the benefit. I spent too much time in a "learn first, then build" mindset before realising that, for me, the better path was often the opposite: build first, then learn from what the work reveals.

That is why I like small experiments, quick results, and finished projects. We make mistakes, discover what we still need to learn, and improve faster when we work from real constraints. Most of what I build and share starts there.

## Me

I am Alexis, a French teacher, a solo builder developing products and services for language teaching, and a programmer involved in the open source community.

## Solving problems

Most of the problems I work on begin with friction I experience myself or see in the people I help.

In teaching, learning, and building, useful work often feels harder than it should. Producing high-quality content takes time, learning can become heavier than necessary, and too many small tasks start competing for attention at once.

That is usually where I begin. I break problems into smaller ones and look for practical solutions for each part, often through programming, but not always. We learn, practice, and build better when cognitive load is lower.

What looks complex is often many simple things put together. When I can make those parts clearer and make them work together, I can create tools that help me and other people. I share many of those tools with the open source community on [GitHub](https://github.com/alnah/) under a liberal and reusable [BSD 3-Clause license](https://opensource.org/license/bsd-3-clause).

## Building systems

When those smaller solutions start fitting together, they become larger systems.

That often means combining scripts, command-line interfaces (CLIs), servers that expose application programming interfaces (APIs), and agentic workflows built with large language models (LLMs) for coding and non-coding tasks. Those systems often become the backbone of the products and services I build for language teaching through web apps, websites, or hybrid websites.

Each time I build something, I want it to be useful first, trustworthy in real use, clear enough that both I and other people can understand it, and extensible enough to connect with other tools over time.

## My work

Some of my public work lives on GitHub. I built these tools to reduce friction in my own work, often by combining agentic workflows, speech models, and structured automation.

### Public

- [`picoloom`](https://github.com/alnah/picoloom) is a CLI for turning Markdown teaching materials, including LLM-assisted drafts and revisions, into polished and reusable PDFs.
- [`panefleet`](https://github.com/alnah/panefleet) is a tmux-native workboard I built to keep multi-agent LLM workflows visible and manageable across panes, sessions, and windows.
- [`things-agent`](https://github.com/alnah/things-agent) is a macOS CLI for turning long LLM conversations into actionable projects, tasks, checklists, and notes in Things 3.
- [`transcript`](https://github.com/alnah/transcript) is a CLI for capturing audio and turning it into Markdown through model-based transcription and structured post-processing.
- [`falaro`](https://github.com/alnah/falaro) is audio production tooling built around TTS models, caching, and incremental assembly for reusable spoken content.

### Commercial

- `fle` is my internal operations stack, combining retrieval, automation, and structured publishing to support individualised teaching on [italki](https://www.italki.com/fr/teacher/8123606).
- `upcoming...` more products, and services for learning French, or building educative content.

## Why this site exists

Useful work often looks clean only after it is finished. In the middle of it, it rarely feels that way.

I want this site to make the process visible, not just the polished result. When people can see the false starts, the iterations, the mistakes, and the small solutions that eventually make something work, it becomes easier for people to apply for their own use case. That approach helped me a lot, and I hope it can help other people by making knowledge more accessible, reusable, and easier to understand.

People arrive here from different kinds of work. Some come for teaching and language learning. Others come for tools, workflows, product thinking, or programming. What connects those paths is the same search for clearer ways to learn, work, and build with less friction.

The site is organised into five categories. You can use the search bar if you are looking for something specific, or browse by category depending on the kind of problem, idea, or workflow that brought you here.

- **thinking**: notes and reflections accessible to everyone
- **teaching**: ideas and practices about learning for teachers and students
- **working**: business operations for people building useful things
- **tooling**: tools, systems, and technologies for programmers
- **building**: what I make, test, and ship in public
